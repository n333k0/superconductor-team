# Discovery Brief

Client slug: `client-example`
Date: 2026-07-03
Owner: Discovery Lead

## Client Snapshot

Northstar Fieldworks sells software and implementation support for organizations coordinating distributed field teams. The company is preparing for an enterprise sales push and needs a clearer position, offer architecture, and deck narrative.

## Engagement Objective

Create a strategy foundation and deck structure that helps enterprise operations buyers understand Northstar as an operational visibility and coordination partner, not just a workflow tool.

## Central Discovery Question

How should Northstar Fieldworks position, package, and present its field operations platform so that enterprise operations leaders believe it can reduce coordination risk and customer escalations despite a crowded market of field service tools and internal workarounds?

## Situation Summary

Northstar appears to have useful operational capabilities, but its story is still feature-led. The likely strategic challenge is not whether field teams need coordination support; it is whether enterprise buyers see Northstar as credible enough to change existing workflows and involve multiple stakeholders.

## Working Hypotheses

| Hypothesis | Evidence Needed | Decision It Affects | Current Confidence |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Enterprise buyers will respond better to "coordination risk" and "operational visibility" than generic workflow language. | Sales notes, buyer language, competitor messaging. | Positioning and deck narrative. | Medium |
| Implementation support can be positioned as adoption assurance rather than custom services. | Onboarding timelines, customer success notes, proof of faster rollout. | Offer architecture. | Medium |
| The status quo is the hardest competitor because buyers already use patched-together tools. | Sales objections and lost deal reasons. | Benchmark and proof strategy. | High |
| Stronger vertical prioritization is needed before enterprise outreach. | Win rate, retention, deal size, implementation complexity by vertical. | Audience priority. | Low |

## Evidence Plan

Market research should investigate:

- Buyer language around field coordination, dispatch visibility, service reliability, and operational reporting.
- Enterprise triggers that make manual workarounds unacceptable.
- Proof expectations for operations executives and IT stakeholders.

Competitor benchmarking should investigate:

- Whether competitors claim broad "field service management" value or more specific operational outcomes.
- How competitors frame implementation, onboarding, integrations, and enterprise readiness.
- Visual conventions that make the category feel generic.

Offering architecture should investigate:

- Whether the current offer should be framed as platform plus launch system plus optimization support.
- Which offer layer reduces the most buying friction.

Client clarification needed:

- Vertical win-rate and retention data.
- Available performance proof.
- Implementation scope and timeline norms.
- Integration requirements.

## Decision Criteria

The final strategy should be judged by:

- It gives sales a clearer executive conversation.
- It makes the software-plus-services model easier to buy.
- It creates a credible distinction from generic field service tools.
- It can be supported by existing or near-term proof.
- It avoids requiring a full rebrand.

## Risks

| Risk | Potential Impact | Mitigation |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Insufficient proof for enterprise claims | Strategy may sound aspirational. | Use proof requirements and caveat unsupported claims. |
| Overly broad audience definition | Deck becomes generic. | Prioritize verticals once client data is available. |
| Implementation appears heavy | Buyers may expect long rollout. | Frame implementation as adoption assurance with clear phases. |

## Handoff

Next agents: Market Researcher, Competitor Benchmark, Offering Architect.

Immediate next action: Complete research and benchmark with attention to buyer language, status quo competition, and implementation framing.
