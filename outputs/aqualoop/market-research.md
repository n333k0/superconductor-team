## Executive summary

Aqualoop appears to be an onsite greywater and alternate-water treatment platform from INTEWA, distributed in North America by EcoVie Water. The product sits in the decentralized water reuse category: systems installed at a building, campus, or local district to collect, treat, and reuse greywater, rainwater, stormwater, condensate, or other alternate sources for non-potable uses such as toilet flushing, irrigation, laundry, drain trap priming, and potentially cooling tower makeup.

The category is moving from sustainability-led pilots into code-driven infrastructure. The strongest near-term adoption signals are in water-stressed, regulation-forward, high-construction-cost markets where large multifamily, mixed-use, hospitality, campus, and commercial projects already use dual plumbing and centralized building operations. San Francisco is the clearest U.S. proof point: new development projects of 100,000 gross square feet or more must install and operate onsite water reuse systems, and projects of 40,000 gross square feet or more must submit water budget calculations.

Aqualoop's marketable edge is certification and technical credibility. Public materials position Aqualoop as a modular membrane bioreactor system using prefiltration, biological treatment, ultrafiltration membranes, backwash/air scouring, control systems, and final UV or residual disinfection. EcoVie claims Aqualoop is certified to NSF/ANSI 350C for commercial greywater reuse and reports third-party disinfection performance of greater than 6-log bacteria and 5-log virus reduction. This matters because regulators, engineers, and owners increasingly want documented pathogen reduction, code references, and operations data, not just high-level sustainability claims.

The main commercial constraint is not market awareness; it is project risk. Buyers need help proving permitting viability, operational responsibility, lifecycle cost, maintenance burden, building integration, and return on investment. Aqualoop should be sold less like a standalone green product and more like a de-risked water infrastructure package for architects, MEP engineers, developers, municipalities, and owners who need code compliance, potable offset, resilience, and ESG performance.

## Category definition

Category: onsite non-potable water reuse, with emphasis on greywater recycling and modular alternate-water treatment for buildings.

Core jobs to be done:

- Reduce potable water demand in buildings.
- Satisfy local onsite reuse ordinances or water budget requirements.
- Support green building certification and sustainability commitments.
- Improve water resilience in drought-prone or infrastructure-constrained areas.
- Lower long-term water and sewer costs where utility rates or connection charges justify investment.
- Enable non-potable end uses with treated onsite sources instead of municipal potable water.

Typical source waters:

- Greywater from showers, bathtubs, hand basins, and sometimes laundry.
- Rainwater, stormwater, foundation drainage, and condensate.
- Selected commercial or industrial process water where chemistry and treatment goals are compatible.

Typical end uses:

- Toilet and urinal flushing.
- Irrigation.
- Clothes washing, where allowed.
- Drain trap priming.
- Cooling tower or process makeup, depending on treatment train, local rules, and owner risk tolerance.

## Market drivers

### 1. Regulation is creating non-discretionary demand

The best opportunities are where onsite reuse is required or strongly incentivized. San Francisco is the leading U.S. local model. Its Onsite Water Reuse Program requires onsite systems for new developments of 100,000 gross square feet or more, while projects of 40,000 gross square feet or more must submit water budget calculations. The city also offers grants that can range from $200,000 to $1,000,000 depending on expected potable offset.

California also has a statewide rulemaking context for onsite treated non-potable water systems. The State Water Resources Control Board describes risk-based standards for onsite treatment and reuse in multifamily residential, commercial, and mixed-use buildings, and notes that existing systems will need to comply within two years of the regulations' effective date.

### 2. Standards are lowering buyer uncertainty

NSF/ANSI 350 provides a recognized framework for onsite greywater and wastewater reuse treatment systems. NSF describes the standard as establishing guidelines for testing onsite greywater and wastewater reuse treatment systems. EPA summaries note that NSF/ANSI 350 covers residential and commercial systems, multiple influent types, and non-potable applications including toilet and urinal flushing and irrigation.

The IAPMO WE-Stand also contains detailed provisions for alternate water source systems, including greywater, onsite treated non-potable water, rainwater, and reclaimed water. This standards layer helps specifiers, code officials, and owners compare solutions and reduces the perception that onsite reuse is a custom science project.

### 3. Water scarcity and infrastructure stress support the business case

EPA frames onsite non-potable water reuse as a response to growing water scarcity and demand for alternative sources. The National Water Reuse Action Plan has expanded since 2020 and continues to support potable and non-potable reuse through technical, institutional, and financial workstreams. This creates favorable air cover for local adoption, federal research funding, and utility-facing education.

### 4. Large buildings have the best economics

Onsite reuse economics generally improve when a project has:

- Large and predictable greywater supply.
- Large non-potable demand.
- New construction or major renovation, reducing retrofit cost.
- Centralized building operations.
- Utility connection charges, capacity charges, or water/sewer rates that make potable offset valuable.
- Local mandates, grants, density incentives, or ESG requirements.

This makes large multifamily, hotels, student housing, senior living, resorts, campuses, public buildings, recreation centers, stadiums, and mixed-use developments better early targets than small retrofits.

### 5. Decentralized resilience is becoming a board-level issue

WateReuse describes onsite systems as a way to provide additional water supply, offset potable demand during drought, manage stormwater, treat wastewater, and support building occupants during floods and earthquakes. The category increasingly connects water savings with resilience, continuity, and infrastructure capacity, which broadens the buyer beyond sustainability teams.

- Mordor Intelligence estimated the water recycle and reuse market at USD 20.6 billion in 2026 and forecast USD 35.0 billion by 2031, an 11.22% CAGR.

These are not direct TAM estimates for building-level greywater systems. A practical segmentation for Aqualoop is:

- Serviceable market: decentralized onsite non-potable reuse systems for buildings and campuses.
- Near-term beachhead: regulation-led and drought-led new construction projects needing certified greywater or alternate-water systems.

## Risks and barriers

- Permitting fragmentation: Requirements vary by city, state, plumbing code, health department, and end use.
- High upfront cost: Economics are project-specific and may be hard to justify without mandates, grants, or high utility charges.
- Operational accountability: Owners may worry about maintenance, sampling, alarms, and noncompliance.
- Retrofit friction: Existing buildings may lack dual plumbing and space for tanks/equipment.
- Public perception: Reuse still faces acceptance barriers, especially when messaging is imprecise about greywater versus blackwater and non-potable versus potable use.
- Competition from low-cost conservation: Efficient fixtures, irrigation controls, and behavior programs may be cheaper in small buildings.
- Source/demand mismatch: Greywater supply and non-potable demand must align, or storage, bypass, and potable makeup reduce savings.
