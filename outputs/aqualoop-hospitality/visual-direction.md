# Visual Direction

Client slug: `aqualoop-hospitality`
Date: 2026-07-03
Visual director: Visual Director Agent

## Strategic Role Of Visuals

The deck should make an invisible infrastructure idea tangible without exaggerating readiness. Visuals should help the audience understand sources, reuse destinations, proof gates, stakeholder ownership, and architecture choices.

## Category Visual Audit Summary

Common visual conventions:

- Blue water imagery.
- Eco/sustainability symbols.
- Pipes, buildings, dashboards, and circular loops.
- Clean technology product renderings.

Conventions to avoid:

- Abstract water splashes that imply proof without substance.
- Generic green-building imagery.
- "Plug-and-play" visuals before installation feasibility is proven.
- Hotel luxury imagery that distracts from operations and engineering.

Useful conventions to keep:

- Building-section diagrams.
- Source-to-use flows.
- Compliance gate diagrams.
- Proof ladders.
- Plain risk/readiness badges.

## Direction Principles

1. Show the building as the system.
2. Make uncertainty visible and useful.
3. Separate public evidence, assumptions, and blocked claims.

## Color Behavior

Use a restrained technical palette: deep charcoal, off-white, muted aqua, slate, and amber/risk accents. Avoid a one-note blue water palette.

## Typography Direction

Use clear executive typography with compact headings and evidence labels. Avoid oversized marketing slogans except on the opening slide.

## Imagery Direction

Use real hotel infrastructure references only when licensed/available. Until then, prioritize diagrams over stock photography.

## Diagram And Data Visualization

- Hotel water-zone map.
- Architecture option matrix.
- Regulatory gate sequence.
- Proof ladder.
- Stakeholder decision map.
- Readiness table.

## Slide Composition

Use dense but readable strategy-deck layouts. Keep evidence labels attached to claims. Use badges for `client_safe`, `evidence_confidence`, and `readiness_gate`.

## Accessibility And Production Constraints

- Do not rely on color alone for readiness.
- Keep contrast high.
- Avoid thin line diagrams below readable size.
- Do not place caveats in tiny footnotes only.
